     Computer vs Manual Adaptation

In OurWord we are attempting to distinguish between Computer and Manual Adaptation.

For experienced Bible translators, computer adaptation is a familiar concept. With programs such as Carla or AdaptIt,

  1. A source text is selected which represents solid exegesis and careful translation
  2. The computer then generates a draft in the target translation.

We would posit that the major innovation here is not "computer," but rather, "adaptation." With adaptation, a decision is being made to use the exegesis, rendering decisions, translation strategies, etc., of a source language; thus saving many, many years of work in a typical New Testament Project. Adaptation frequently cuts the time to produce a translation at least in half.

An adaptation project still requires all of the translation processes that would accompany a first draft in a non-adaptation project. Thus the draft must be team checked, advisor checked, community checked, back translated, consultant checked, etc. Thus twenty years may be cut to severn to ten, but the necessary down-stream checking prevents an adaptation from published instantaneously.

Thus if Adaptation can be defined to use a source text as the basis of translation, then

  • Computer Adaptation involves having the computer produce the first draft by exchanging words from source langauge into target language
  • Manual Adaptation involves having a translator produce the first draft by translating directly from the source language.

Surprisingly, trials have demonstrated that there is no measurable time difference between Manual vs Xomputer adaptation.The reason is that while Computer Adaptation can quickly produce something in a target language, that raw draft almost always requires a first pass to edit out the computerisms. Thus Computer Adaptation plus Raw Draft Processing seems to take about the same amount of time as a Manual Adaptation's single translation pass.

There are numerous factors to consider when choosing between Computer and Manual Adaptation, including:

  • If languages are so distance that speakers of the target cannot understand the source, then manual adaptation is not possible. However, a good linguist equipped with Carla can sometimes make computer adaptation viable.
  • If languages are so very similar that only a handful of words are changing, such that little post-processing is required, then a Computer Adaptation strategy using Adapt It will probably be the fastest way to get a good result.
  • In Manual Adaptation, certain sociolinguistic factors are addressed. For example, in Computer Adaptation the receiptients often feel that they are making a "copy" of the translation of a language that for whatever reason, they disdain. With Manual Adaptation this usually does not happen, because the translator feels like he is starting out with a blank slate and creating his own translation.
  • With Computer Adaptation, the translator is learning a different skill set: that of correcting the computer. Thus if the computer has produced stilted language, or has transferred strangenesses from the source language, the translator is learning the skills to clean these up. With Manual Adaptation, on the other hand, the translator merely translates, and thus can be trained solely in translation skills.
  • With Computer Adaptation, you must teach the translator to overcome that feeling of "if the computer suggested it, then it must be right." It is common for new and experienced users alike to put too much trust in the computer.

If you are unsure which to choose, we suggest that you conduct a trial. Choose passages of similar genre, and let the translator work with both approaches. Your key metric should be about which produces the best translation in your situation, especially taking into account the types of things that must be fixed under each method as the translation proceeds through the various downstream checks. Thus ideally your trail would involve taking the results from both types of adaptation all the way through the translation process.
