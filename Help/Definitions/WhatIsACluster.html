     What is a Cluster?

A common misconception is that a cluster, at least with regard to the feasibility of Bible translation, must be defined as languages that are linguistically related. With manual adaptation, however, we have found that linguistic similarity is not a crucial factor; in fact languages can be of completely different families, —as long as the translators are bilingual in the source translation. There are numerous other factors, though, which do indeed impact how a cluster is defined and how the work might be carried out to most favorably impact Scripture Use. In the following Dr. Charles E. Grimes describes clusters as seen in Timor, Indonesia.

I suggest that there are various kinds of “clusters”, and several aspects of “clusters” often converge in a given situation. Each type can be expoited as an opportunity. A “cluster” may be:

1. Closely related languages.

In the Timor region, this is of little significance over-all, and of only secondary relevance for 6 translations on the island of Rote. The translators in that dialect-language chain are basically not even interested in what their neighbouring language translations are saying. In fact there are social and cultural reasons that make some of them refuse to even consider how neighbouring languages might phrase something. It is the advisors not the MTT’s who feel it is worth checking how something has been translated in a closely related language.

2. Geographical proximity.

E.g. same island, region, province, country. This is logistically relevant for several “clusters” in the Timor region.

3. Political cohesion.

E.g. they fall under the same administrative control and share the same political or security considerations. In the Timor context, all the languages in East Timor share certain political considerations, even though they may not be related linguistically. In west Timor Dhao relates politically to Rote, whereas linguistically it is closest to Hawu. However Hawu falls administratively under Kupang. This impacts a number of things in the total picture of doing translation.

4. Church dominance.

In the Timor context we relate to 3 different synods in mainline protestant churches, each with different geographical scope. The Catholics have a completely separate hierarchy, and so we also need to relate to several different bishops and archbishops. Some of the bishops relate to each other, and some do not. There is no relationship between the Catholic system and the protestant system. Among the smaller denominations, there are several that are strictly urban and have no vision for nor interest in vernacular translations. Historically, in places like the top end of Australia, Vanuatu and parts of Indonesia, churches or mission societies have actually carved up a map and staked out territories as “belonging” to different churches to missionize. If there is to be any real scripture use of our translations, in those regions we MUST relate to those churches in unique and appropriate ways. GMIT [the largest synod, with 1.3 million members] church leaders have specifically requested that vernacular translations of the Bible done in their geographically scattered “region” not diverge greatly from each other in their exegetical choices. This makes using a consultant checked Front Translation that has had the involvement of senior church leaders of good standing a strategic necessity. This mandate from the church is a very real consideration when interacting with translation teams, advisors, and consultants (Many SIL consultants are not used to working within these parameters.)

5. Organisational dominance.

In some situations one mission or Bible agency will back off from working in a language or area, because another mission or Bible agency is or has already worked there. In a number of cases this kind of thing can influence where one works, how one translates, and the expectations and translation style of so-called ‘experienced’ local translators and the community. This presents special challenges where the first organisation did not do a good job with such things as usable orthographies, careful exegesis, producing an understandable translation, or relating to churches, but where the church or community is now asking for a usable translation from a different organisation. There are often "clusters" of these languages.

6. Multilingual situation with a shared language of wider communication.

This last one is the situation that best characterises what is crucial to what is happening in Timor in Bible translation. It is also a sociolinguistic situation found repeatedly around the world. The fact that speakers of these languages share a language of wider communication is of far greater significance than linguistic relatedness of the vernaculars. It allows us to make a first rate Front Translation in the LWC, and local communities can use it to do an MTT vernacular translation whether they speak Austronesian languages or Papuan languages. It is also what makes the OurWord software so useful in our context—it does not need the languages to work similarly—it only needs the translators to be able to use the Front Translation in the LWC.

by Dr. Charles E. Grimes, 10 March 2005
