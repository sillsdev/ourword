     What is a "Front Translation?"

In OurWord we generally refer to the source translation as the Front Translation. Computer adaptation software generally uses the term "source" for this; but we are using "front" in order to make a point about quality. The computer adage "Garbage In Equals Garbage Out" applies to translation: if you start with a poor-quality source translation, then you will be constantly fighting an uphill battle. I have seen first-hand far too many people make excuses for using a poor source translation under the misguided assumption that time will be saved. Sadly, this approach can lead to translations that in terms of exegesis and naturalness, are beyond repair. I would thus strongly suggest that you are far better off drafting from scratch than you are adapting from a poor source translation.

Following in Charles Grimes' definition of a good-quality front translation.

Simply put: a Front Translation is source text in a language accessible to local translators. However, when we are honest, many national language translations are inadequate to use as source texts for local translators.

So what we mean by a Front Translation is: A fully consultant checked translation in a regional language of wider communication that is used as an accessible source text for local translators to draft from into their own languages. The Front Translation has:

  • carefully interpreted the meaning of the original language (Greek or Hebrew) texts (= exegesis)
  • carefully worked through, unpacked, and tested key Biblical terms, keeping in mind regionally relevant issues
  • carefully repackaged the higher level discourse structure so that it uses appropriate patterns for stringing together information widespread in the region for:
    • background-foreground information
    • chronology or logic
    • referent-tracking mechanisms, such as pronominalisation strategies
    • shifts in time, location, or action
  • care taken to get the connectors right (accurate and natural conjunctions)
  • care taken in regionally relevant and anthropologically informed lexical choice issues to make sure the right word is used from a group of similar words
  • care taken to carefully and appropriately employ idioms and figures of speech widespread in the region
  • had at least two community checks with groups of uninitiated native speakers of the language of wider communication to ensure clarity and naturalness
  • addressed implicit to explicit issues in response to uninitiated native speaker answers, and in accord with discourse patterns of languages in the region
  • where possible and appropriate, the front translation has had the involvement of senior church leaders, and they are on-board with the over-all approach to translation as well as the style

by Charles E. Grimes, PhD, December 2004
